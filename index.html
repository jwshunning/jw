The Shunned Speak Out

Life Inside the Jehovah's Witnesses and After

Stories of loss, abuse, and hope

When Anna told her five-year-old son, Karama, that his father, Robert McCoy Sr., would be resurrected from the dead, he did everything to make sure he was there to see it happen.

If he was obedient, he would get to see the earth transformed into a lush paradise, Anna said.

His father would be there- along with lions, tigers, and panda bears docile enough for children to play with. His family would be together again. Best of all, this time everyone would be immortal and without flaw.

On December 5, 2017, 37 years after Robert’s murder, ten red letters perched in view of the Brooklyn Bridge transformed to crimson against the night sky one last time. The following day, the 15-foot letters which spelled WATCHTOWER, were removed after more than 40 years on the city’s skyline.

For Robert’s widow, Anna, and more than 8 million people worldwide, the Watchtower sign and the series of buildings it marked was something of a mecca. They called it Bethel, a word that means “House of God.”

The Watch Tower, Bible, and Tract Society, the incorporation of the Jehovah’s Witnesses, sold their headquarters that bore the iconic Watchtower sign to Donald Trump son-in-law Jared Kushner’s development company for $340 million dollars last year. Another property on the same block was sold in August for $18 million, and another for $87.5 million.

The corporation has paid millions in settlements to victims of sexual abuse, but a Pew Research study states that nearly half of Jehovah’s Witnesses in the U.S. live on less than $30,000 a year. Only nine percent have an undergraduate degree, the lowest percentage among similar religious groups.

Jehovah’s Witnesses may only call to mind a set of rigid rules or an unexpected knock on the door from friendly Bible toters claiming to be neighbors,  but their practice of shunning isn’t as innocuous. It is called disfellowshipping, and the application stings hundreds of ex-Jehovah’s Witnesses and their families every year. The reasons vary, but the emotional headwinds for former members as they recover from the trauma of losing the only community they have known rests in the uncomfortable terrain between the grief of death and the joy of discovering a new existence.

IN THE BEGINNING

Charles Taze Russell, a restorationist Christian minister, founded the group in Pennsylvania in the 1880s. They went by the name “Bible Students” as they distributed material written by Russell and printed by his publishing company, “The Watch Tower Bible and Tract Society.”

Using a literal interpretation of the Bible, Russell predicted the world would end in a war called Armageddon. He also used a mathematical formulation based on an ancient Hebrew calendar to determine that 1874 was the year when Christ would return to rule. Christ’s rule would signal the beginning of Armageddon, which would come to pass in 1914.

When Russell died, he was succeeded by an eccentric attorney named Joseph Rutherford, nicknamed “Judge.” After Russell’s prediction for the end of the world did not come to pass, Rutherford made a new prediction. Instead of 1874, 1914 would be the year. The former trial lawyer also believed Old Testament prophets would return to earth and live in a mansion he built in San Diego called Beth Sarim.

Six years after Russell’s death, Rutherford proposed a name change to roaring crowd at a convention on a warm day in early autumn 1922. The Bible Students became Jehovah’s Witnesses.

THE ART OF THE BUBBLE

They do not celebrate any holidays, including birthdays, Christmas, and Easter. They do not salute the flag, serve in the military, or accept blood transfusions. But these distinct rules are really the only things that set Jehovah’s Witnesses apart.

No bloodline ties them, and they don’t don a particular style of dress. They don’t live in cordoned communities. In the U.S., they are almost equally racially and ethnically mixed among whites, African Americans, and Latinos.  

But the isolation is insidious. Articles in Watchtower and Awake! Magazines published by the organization warn members to limit association with non-witnesses, including family members. Separation happens naturally because the time commitment to door-to-door preaching, along with employment or school, does not leave room for much else.

“Jehovah’s Witnesses have perfected the art of keeping you in a mental bubble. They don’t ask people to live in separate quarters, but they have ways of keeping you isolated anyway,” said Ruben Ortiz, a former witness from the Bronx.   

A KNOCK ON THE DOOR

There are approximately 8.3 million Jehovah’s Witnesses worldwide, and they have preaching missions in 240 locations around the globe. Here’s how a person usually gets involved:

1. A Jehovah’s Witness knocks on your door. You like what you hear and invite him/her back to learn more.

2. The witness returns and offers you a Bible study. You accept.

3. You begin to attend meetings at the Kingdom Hall, the JW place of worship.

4. After some time, you are allowed to go out and do some preaching of your own. When you go out and preach, you become what is known as a “publisher.” You are expected to put in around 3 hours per week.

5. You’re a good publisher and make your hourly requirements and attend all of the weekly meetings, so you decide to get baptized. You have made a lifelong commitment to the Jehovah’s Witnesses to practice all of their teachings and fulfill the tenets of service.

Besides the people who converted in the way outlined above, about a third of Jehovah’s Witnesses were indoctrinated as children, also known as being “born in” the faith.

If a Jehovah’s Witness commits what is deemed “a serious sin” and does not express sufficient repentance, they are to be excommunicated, according to an article published in the August 2015 Watchtower magazine. The article, which calls disfellowshipping “a loving provision” offers the most recent explanation of the practice, which was instituted in 1952.  

Serious sins include engaging in premarital sex, smoking cigarettes, and marital infidelity, but there is no exhaustive list. Offenses encompass acts that range from watching pornography to getting a tattoo.

When a person has confessed the act, but does not appear to be sufficiently repentant- a subjective process, up to the discernment of congregation elders-the person is then disfellowshipped.

AT HOME AND DISFELLOWSHIPPED

If an adult is disfellowshipped, the repercussions are straightforward. But when a young person who still lives with parents is excommunicated, the family often ends up dancing an invisible line drawn between the rooms of the family home, around the dining table, in the hallways. The intention is to make to the offending person feel shame, but the rituals of family life and the practicalities of living under the same roof complicate the mission.

Disfellowshipping among Jehovah’s Witnesses is a means to maintain a strong sense of membership, according to religious sociologists, Rodney Stark and Laurence Iannaccone.

In their 1997 paper, “Why The Jehovah’s Witnesses Grow So Rapidly: A Theoretical Application,” published in the Journal of Contemporary Religion, they praise Jehovah’s Witnesses as “the fastest growing sect in the world.”

Their projections for the growth rate of Jehovah’s Witnesses were later discredited, but their research on what they call “the success of the organization” is still largely cited among religious studies scholars. They attribute the growth of the organization, in part, to the practice of disfellowshipping.

Stark and Iannoccone argue that If an organization is strict, it increases the value of membership by weeding out “free-loaders,” their term for people who do not fulfill their share of the work membership requires. Disfellowshipping creates a sense of solidarity and members know that their commitment has value, they state.  

But it is not only people who do not carry out their share of the required ministry who are shunned.

Karen Wilson, 55, from Spokane Washington, was a pioneer, a Jehovah’s Witness who does the preaching work full-time, when she was disfellowshipped. Wilson was physically abused by her husband. When she went to the elders, instead of the police, she was told that she should be a more “submissive” wife.

“A lightbulb went off in my head. Why would God have created me, if I was only supposed to be beaten up by my husband?”, said Wilson.

Wilson was an active and devoted Jehovah’s Witness who shunned her mother because it was mandated by the organization, yet she was not protected from shunning when she escaped an abusive spouse.

THE WAY FORWARD

Former Jehovah’s Witnesses have carved out roads to recovery from the psychological damage of being in the organization. Social media groups and websites dedicated to helping ex Jehovah’s Witnesses can be found all over the internet, buzzing with heart wrenching stories of families torn apart and users offering words of encouragement to each other.

There are also stories of joy. This year, Deb D’Agostino’s mother, a 99-year-old Jehovah’s Witness, decided to celebrate her birthday for the first time.

Dan Adams fell in love with another former Jehovah’s Witness, Amy, who he met in an online recovery group. They got married last year.
